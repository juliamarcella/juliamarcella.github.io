01 · Health & Productivity · Featured
Does pain affect how much someone can work?
"And if we could predict it — what would HR teams do differently?"
Live Dashboard ↗ GitHub
The question
How much do menstrual symptoms actually affect workplace productivity?
Millions of people experience cycle-related symptoms at work, but most companies have no data on the impact. This project set out to measure it.
The analysis
Pain level, flow, lifestyle habits, hormones — what matters most?
Analysed data from 2,000 users across cycle logs, hormone readings, lifestyle habits (sleep, exercise, diet) and productivity metrics including work hours lost, mood, and energy.
The answer
Pain is the single strongest driver — and it can be predicted.
Pain level consistently explained more variation in productivity loss than cycle phase, diet, or lifestyle combined. A machine learning model can estimate hours lost with 75% accuracy.

02 · E-Commerce · Customer Analytics
Who are your best customers — and what will they spend next?
"If you knew which customers were about to go quiet, what would you do differently?"
GitHub ↗ Power BI (in progress)
The question
A UK retailer has thousands of transactions. But who are the customers behind them?
The business had transaction data but no customer intelligence. No segments, no lifetime value model, no way to know who to focus on.
The analysis
RFM segmentation, clustering, cohort analysis, and a predictive model for future revenue
Cleaned a full year of retail transactions, engineered customer behaviour metrics, identified 3 distinct segments, and built a model to predict what each customer will spend next.
The answer
3 clear customer types — each needing a different strategy
VIP customers (frequent, high-spend, recent), Core customers (moderate engagement), and At-Risk customers (formerly active, now dormant). Each segment has a different product preference and revenue potential.
🛒
Best-selling ≠ most profitable
The top 10 products by quantity sold and the top 10 by revenue are completely different lists. Volume and value don't align.
→ For business: promotional strategy should be built on revenue contribution, not units sold — otherwise you're promoting the wrong products
👥
3 customer segments with distinct behaviours
K-Means clustering identified VIP, Core, and At-Risk groups — each with different recency, frequency, monetary profiles and product preferences.
→ For business: personalised campaigns per segment outperform blanket promotions. VIPs need retention. At-risk need re-engagement. Core need activation.
📈
Future revenue can be predicted with 75% accuracy
A Random Forest model using RFM + behavioural features (avg order value, days active, unique products) predicts each customer's next spend.
→ For business: prioritise retention budget on high-predicted-value customers before they churn — not after
📉
Cancellation rate is healthy — under 1%
After flagging all negative-quantity transactions as cancellations, the rate was negligible as a share of total revenue.
→ For business: order fulfilment is performing well. Investment should go into growth and retention, not return prevention
📅
Customer value compounds over time
Cohort and CLV analysis shows how revenue per customer grows across acquisition months — some cohorts significantly outperform others.
→ For business: knowing which acquisition months produce the most valuable long-term customers helps focus marketing spend

03 · Sports & Gender · Data Storytelling
How long did women have to wait to compete?
"Rugby admitted women 116 years after men. What does the rest of the data say?"
Live Dashboard ↗ GitHub
The question
Has the Olympics actually become more equal — or does it just look that way?
Over a century of Olympic history, analysed through gender equity. When were women first allowed to compete in each sport? How has that changed?
The analysis
Over 163,000 athlete records across every Olympic edition from 1896 to 2022
Engineered a "gender entry gap" metric for every discipline, tracked participation growth over time, and mapped medal distribution by gender and country.
The answer
Progress is real — but the numbers tell a more complicated story
Women's participation has grown sharply since the 1980s. But they still represent only 28.3% of athletes and 29.3% of medals — and some sports waited over a century to include them.
⏳
Some sports waited over 100 years
Rugby: 116 years. Wrestling: 108 years. Boxing: 108 years. Weightlifting: 104 years. These are not historical footnotes — they're within living memory.
→ The data makes an invisible structural issue visible. That's the most important thing analysis can do.
📈
Sharp growth since the 1980s
Women's participation grew dramatically from the 1980s onward — driven by policy changes and new event additions.
→ Policy decisions have measurable effects on participation. The data can show what works and how fast.
🏅
Medal share mirrors participation
Women hold 29.3% of medals, men 65.2%. The gap isn't just about opportunity — it's about the number of events available to each gender.
→ Increasing women's events directly increases medal opportunity. Participation and equity are structurally linked.
