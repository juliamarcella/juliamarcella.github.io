01 — Health & Productivity
Menstrual Cycle & Workplace Productivity
"Does pain actually affect how much work someone can do — and can we predict it?"
GitHub Dashboard Presentation
The Problem
Companies have no data on how cycle symptoms affect performance

Millions of people experience menstrual symptoms at work every month — pain, fatigue, mood changes — but most organisations have no way to measure the real impact. Without data, there's no case for better policies.

The Analysis
2,000 users · 17,972 cycle logs · symptoms, lifestyle & productivity

Analysed hormone readings, symptom severity, and lifestyle habits (sleep, exercise, diet, stress) against productivity metrics — hours of work lost, mood scores, and energy levels. Then built a machine learning model to predict productivity loss from symptoms alone.

The Answer
Pain is the #1 driver — and it's predictable with 75% accuracy

Pain level explained more productivity loss than cycle phase, diet, or lifestyle combined. The Random Forest model estimates hours lost based on symptom inputs — enabling proactive planning rather than reactive damage control.

3.4 hrs
Nearly half a working day lost on high-pain phases.
Model accuracy
75%
Predicts work hours lost from pain, stress, energy & flow level.
Pain by flow level
3.3 → 7.3

PMS prevalence
51.7%
Over half of users report PMS symptoms — the majority, not the minority.
What this means for a business
Flexible policies targeting high-pain days have more impact than generic wellness programmes — the data shows exactly when people are most affected.
HR teams can use the predictive model to adjust workload proactively — before hours are lost, not after.
51.7% of users experience PMS — any workplace policy that ignores this is already missing the majority.
The 5-page interactive Looker Studio dashboard lets non-technical stakeholders explore insights themselves, no analyst required.

02 — E-Commerce · Customer Analytics
E-Commerce Customer Segmentation & CLV
"Who are your most valuable customers — and what will they spend next?"
GitHub Power BI Dashboard
The Problem
A UK retailer had 375K transactions — but no idea who their customers were

The business had revenue data but no customer intelligence. No segments, no way to tell a loyal customer from a one-time buyer, and no basis for deciding where to focus marketing spend. All customers were treated the same.

The Analysis
RFM segmentation · K-Means clustering · CLV prediction · cohort analysis

Built Recency, Frequency, and Monetary (RFM) metrics for 4,121 customers, then applied K-Means clustering to identify 3 behavioural segments. A Random Forest model predicts future revenue per customer. Cohort analysis tracks how customer value builds month by month.

The Answer
3 customer types — each worth a completely different strategy

VIP customers (0.15%) average €21,300 spend each. Core customers (72.87%) are the reliable backbone. At-Risk customers (26.98%) haven't purchased in 200+ days and need re-engaging before they're gone. The Power BI dashboard makes this visible in real time.

What this means for a business
Losing one VIP costs as much as losing 70 at-risk customers — prioritise retention spend where it has the most impact.
Promotional strategy built on volume leads to the wrong products — revenue contribution should drive campaigns, not units sold.
The CLV model identifies high-value customers before they churn — not after they've already gone quiet.
The Power BI dashboard gives marketing, sales, and leadership real-time segment visibility — no analyst needed each time.

03 — Sports & Gender · Data Storytelling
Women in the Olympic Games
"How long did women have to wait — and where does inequality still hide?"
GitHub Dashboard
The Problem
120 years of Olympic progress — but how much has actually changed?

The Olympics is often held up as a symbol of progress. But behind the headline numbers, how much has women's access to competition actually improved — and in which sports did the gap take longest to close?

The Analysis
163,000+ athlete records · 1896 to 2022 · gender entry gap metric

Engineered a "gender entry gap" for every discipline — years between men's and women's first appearance. Tracked participation growth by decade, mapped medal distribution by gender and country, and built an interactive dashboard with filters for year, season, sport, and country.

The Answer
Progress is real — but some sports waited over a century

Women's participation grew sharply from the 1980s onward. But in 2022, women still represented only 28.3% of athletes and 29.3% of medals. Rugby took 116 years. Wrestling 108. These gaps are not historical footnotes — they happened within living memory.

Women's medal share

Medal share mirrors participation — more events means more medals.

1980s
Participation grew sharply after the 1980s — driven by policy changes and new events.
Why this analysis matters
Quantifying inequality is the first step to addressing it — the data makes an invisible structural issue visible.
Policy decisions have measurable effects: adding women's events directly increases participation and medal opportunity.
Organisations using sports sponsorship or diversity reporting can benchmark progress against 120 years of real historical data.
The interactive dashboard gives media, researchers, and policymakers a self-serve tool — no data team required.
